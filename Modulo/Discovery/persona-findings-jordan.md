# Persona Findings: Jordan (Crypto-Curious Holder)
**Date:** 2026-04-04
**Evaluator:** Peggy (BakedUX engagement agent), embodying Jordan
**Sources evaluated:** modulo.finance (live site), mobile app screenshots (6 screens)

---

## Summary verdict

Jordan would probably not sign up today. She would read the homepage with cautious interest — the "broken DeFi" problem section would resonate — but the solution never meets her where she is. She holds BTC and ETH on Coinbase. The product talks to people who already have self-custodial wallets. The mobile app looks clean but shows a portfolio of assets she doesn't recognise (WBTC, USDC stacks) in a UI that gives her no instruction. She would close the tab impressed but uncertain, and tell herself she'll look again "when it's more ready."

That's the verdict. Everything below is the detail.

---

## 1. First impressions of the website

**Jordan's internal monologue on landing:**

"Ok, dark design. Feels serious. 'One vault, every chain' — I don't know what that means exactly but it sounds powerful. Let me keep reading."

The hero is fine. The tagline is short, which Jordan appreciates. "Borrow, stake, lend, and trade — instantly and securely — across dozens of chains. One app, no bridges, no wallet switching." She doesn't know what bridging is, but she's heard it's complicated, so "no bridges" feels like a relief even if she doesn't fully understand it. The CTA ("Join Modulo") is clear enough.

**The problem section lands well — then the solution loses her.**

The "broken DeFi experience" walkthrough is Modulo's strongest piece of copy. Jordan reads the step-by-step — "depositing BTC onto an exchange, trading it into WBTC, withdrawing to a self-custodial ERC20 wallet, funding that wallet with ETH for gas…" — and feels genuine recognition. *That's exactly why I haven't done anything.* "Seasoned users find it messy. New users don't even try." She feels seen.

Then the solution section says: "Fund → BTC from Any Wallet → Fund Modulo → Handles swaps & bridging → 1-click borrow → USDC."

Her reaction: "OK but... my BTC is on Coinbase. Is that 'any wallet'? What does 'fund Modulo' mean — do I send them my coins? And then what do I get back? USDC? I don't want USDC, I want yield."

The solution is written for someone who already thinks in DeFi primitives. Jordan doesn't. She needs the solution reframed as: "You have BTC on Coinbase. Connect it. We show you what you could earn. You tap once."

**Features section — mixed bag.**

- "Native Asset Support — No wrapping or bridging." Jordan: "Good. I don't know what wrapping is but it sounds like something I'd mess up."
- "Non-custodial by Design — Your keys, your control." Jordan: "What keys? I don't have keys. I have a Coinbase account." This is a red flag for her. It implies she needs to have already made the leap to self-custody that she hasn't made.
- "Modern Login + Recovery — Passkeys, not seed phrases." Jordan: "Oh thank god. This is the first thing that made me feel safe."
- "Fixed-rate Borrowing — Use any asset, even tokenized RWAs." Jordan: "I don't want to borrow. I want to earn. And what's an RWA?" This feature isn't for her and the label doesn't help.
- "Smart Automation — Borrow on dips, deploy on moves." Jordan: "This sounds like it's for traders. That's not me."

**Functionality section — interesting but abstract.**

"Earn native yield by supplying or staking into Modulo collateral pools." Jordan: "OK that's what I want. But what does 'native yield' mean? What rate? What's a collateral pool?" No numbers. No comparison. She has no way to evaluate whether this is better than leaving it on Coinbase.

"Receive any asset by providing your telegram/whatsapp/discord username." Jordan: "That's actually wild in a good way. That's the one thing on this whole page that felt genuinely new and human."

**Architecture section — she skims it.**

Four layers: Wallet, Logic, Protocol, Integration. She reads the labels. She doesn't understand them. She moves on. This section is for builders and investors, not for her.

**"Why Modulo matters" — almost works.**

"Over $2 trillion in crypto — BTC, XRP, DOGE — sits idle, sidelined from DeFi." Jordan: "That's me. That's literally my £3,500." Then she reads the user types: Traders, Stakers, Institutions, Degens. She is none of these. There is no "holders like me who just want to earn something without becoming a crypto expert." She doesn't see herself in the product's own self-description.

**Final CTA — honest but slightly alarming.**

"No more lost assets from broken bridges or seed phrases." Jordan: "Wait, assets can get lost? Should I be worried about that?" The copy is trying to reassure but the mention of "lost assets" plants a seed of anxiety.

**Footer disclaimer — the trust killer.**

"Modulo is a non-custodial platform for accessing decentralized financial protocols. Use at your own risk." Jordan: "Use at your own risk. Great. Really inspiring confidence there." The legal disclaimer reads as an organisation covering itself, not as an organisation standing behind its product. Jordan understands why it's there but it lands badly as the last thing she reads.

## 2. What works for Jordan

1. **The problem diagnosis.** The broken DeFi walkthrough is excellent. Jordan feels genuinely understood. Keep this and make it even more specific to her situation (BTC on Coinbase is the canonical example).

2. **"Passkeys, not seed phrases."** This is Jordan's single biggest fear addressed directly. She would highlight this if she could. It needs to be more prominent — possibly in the hero.

3. **"No bridges, no wallet switching."** She doesn't fully understand it but it sounds like friction removed. She's for it.

4. **Username-based receiving.** The telegram/whatsapp/discord username idea genuinely delights her. It's human, it's legible, it's different. She'd tell a friend about it.

5. **Visual design.** Dark, clean, professional. It does not look like a scam. It doesn't look like it was made in 2018. This matters more than it might seem — Jordan uses visual cues heavily to assess trust.

6. **"Join the Waitlist" framing.** Low commitment. She can express interest without sending any money. This is correct for where she is.

---

## 3. What confuses or scares Jordan

1. **"Non-custodial" appears without explanation.** Jordan has coins on Coinbase. Non-custodial implies she needs to move them to a wallet she controls. That's the exact step she hasn't been able to make. The word appears twice without ever being explained in plain English. If she has to figure out self-custody to use Modulo, she's already lost.

2. **No yield numbers.** She came to find out if she can earn more than leaving coins on Coinbase. The site never tells her a rate, a range, or even a comparison. "Earn native yield" is too abstract. "Earn up to X% on your BTC, compared to 0% sitting idle" would change everything.

3. **"Use at your own risk."** Mentioned above. This lands hard at the end of a journey that was otherwise building trust.

4. **"WBTC," "RWA," "collateral pools," "tokenized."** Jargon appears without glossary or tooltips. Jordan's patience for googling things she doesn't understand is low — she's already done this before and it didn't end well.

5. **No answer to: "What happens to my actual BTC?"** She wants to know her coins are safe. The site explains the architecture but not the safety. What are the risks? What happens if Modulo disappears? Is she insured? Is there a lock-up? She has no way to answer these questions from the site.

6. **The user types don't include her.** Traders. Stakers. Institutions. Degens. She's a holder. She doesn't staking yet — that's what she's trying to learn. This is an exclusion Jordan feels acutely.

7. **No human face on the company.** The site mentions "© 2025 Modulo Finance, LLC" and links to a Careers page, but there's no team page, no founder story, no faces. For someone assessing whether this is a scam, this is a gap. The kickoff notes Anthony Merriman (of Talos) is a founder — that's a credibility signal that isn't on the site at all.

8. **"Smart Automation — Borrow on dips, deploy on moves."** This sounds like trading strategy copy. Jordan isn't trading. She's mildly worried this product is actually for traders and she's been browsing the wrong site.

---

## 4. What's missing that Jordan needs

**The single most important missing thing:** A plain-English answer to "I have BTC on Coinbase, what do I actually do, and what do I actually get?"

Beyond that, in priority order:

- **An illustrative rate.** Even approximate. "BTC holders in our beta earned X% annually." Give her a number to anchor on.
- **A risk explanation.** What are the actual risks? What's the worst case? Is there insurance? This doesn't have to be long — two sentences of honest risk framing would build more trust than hiding it.
- **A lock-up answer.** Can she get her BTC back at any time? Or is there a lock-up period? She needs to know she can exit.
- **A "how it works in 3 steps" for her specifically.** Something like: "1. Connect your Coinbase. 2. We show you what you could earn. 3. You decide." Not technical. Not jargon. Just the actual flow.
- **A team / about page.** Faces and names. LinkedIn links. Something that says "real people built this."
- **A plain-English FAQ.** "Is my crypto safe?" "What if I change my mind?" "Do I need a wallet?" These are Jordan's questions. They're almost certainly everyone's questions.
- **Support access.** Where do you go if something goes wrong? There's no chat, no email, no phone number visible. This is a hard stop for Jordan — she needs to know there's a human somewhere.

---

## 5. Would Jordan sign up for the waitlist?

**Probably yes, reluctantly.**

The "Join the Waitlist" ask is low enough commitment that she'd probably do it — she'd enter her email, tell herself she'd investigate more before anything went live, and then forget about it for three months.

But she wouldn't be excited. She'd be hedging.

The site has not earned her trust. It's earned her interest. That's a real difference. Interest without trust means she signs up and then needs a lot more persuasion before she does anything with her actual coins.

If Modulo wants Jordan to be genuinely engaged when the product launches — not just an email in a list — the waitlist experience needs to do the trust-building work the site doesn't do. An onboarding email sequence that answers her actual questions (in plain English, with rates, with risk disclosure, with a human face) would close the gap.

## 7. Priority issues that MUST be fixed to win Jordan's trust

These are ordered by impact on her likelihood to sign up and, eventually, to use the product with real money.

### P0 — Blocking trust entirely

**1. Resolve the WBTC contradiction.**
The homepage says "No wrapping or bridging." The app shows Wrapped Bitcoin throughout. Jordan will notice this. It will feel like a lie. Either the UI needs to show "Bitcoin (held natively)" with WBTC being a purely internal abstraction that users never see, or the marketing copy needs to be more precise about what "native" means. This is a credibility killer.

**2. Show yield numbers.**
"Earn native yield" means nothing without a number. Jordan came to find out if this is worth her time. Without a rate — even approximate, even a range — she has no basis for a decision. An illustrative APY, clearly labelled as an estimate, changes everything.

**3. Answer the Coinbase question explicitly.**
Jordan's BTC and ETH are on Coinbase. The homepage never directly addresses this. It needs one sentence: "If your crypto is on Coinbase, Kraken, or Binance — you can connect directly. No self-custody setup required." (Or, if that's not true yet, be honest about what is required.)

### P1 — Significant friction for Jordan

**4. Make the username receive flow the primary flow.**
The Receive screen buries the username/exchange connection behind a QR code and wallet address. Jordan's safest path (connecting from Coinbase) should be the first and most prominent option. The QR code should be secondary or hidden in an "advanced" option.

**5. Add an onboarding state to the app.**
Every screen assumes the user already has assets in Modulo. Jordan's first session would be empty. What does she see? Where does she start? The product needs an empty state that guides her to her first action.

**6. Add fee disclosure to the Swap screen.**
Before Jordan completes any swap, she needs to see: the exchange rate, the fee, and what she'll receive. This is basic transactional hygiene but also a trust requirement for someone who's afraid of getting the wrong thing.

**7. Label recipients with names, not addresses.**
In the Select Recipient screen, recent contacts shown as hex strings are anxiety-inducing. Allow users to name their recipients. Show the most recently used name prominently. Make the ENS/username path the obvious first choice.

### P2 — Important for retention and conversion

**8. Add team/founder credibility to the website.**
A photo and one sentence about Anthony Merriman (Talos background) would materially increase trust with Jordan. She uses human faces to decide if something is legitimate.

**9. Make "passkeys, not seed phrases" a hero-level message.**
This is the single sentence that removes Jordan's biggest fear. It's buried in a feature list. Bring it to the top. Lead with it.

**10. Rewrite the footer disclaimer.**
"Use at your own risk" is accurate but corrosive. Consider: "Modulo is a non-custodial platform. Your assets remain under your control. Read our risk disclosure to understand how it works." Same information, different posture.

**11. Add a plain-English FAQ.**
Answer: "Is my crypto safe?" / "What if I want to withdraw?" / "Do I need a crypto wallet?" / "What are the fees?" / "Can I lose everything?" Three sentences each. Jordan will read this before she sends any money.

## 8. Jordan's ideal onboarding flow

If Jordan could design her own onboarding, it would go something like this:

**Website:**
> "You have BTC or ETH sitting on an exchange. Here's what you could be earning. Connect your account in two minutes — no wallet setup, no seed phrases, no gas fees. We handle everything."
> [See what you could earn] → [Join the waitlist]

**Waitlist confirmation email:**
- Who Modulo is (one human paragraph, with faces)
- One plain-English explanation of how yield works
- One number: estimated annual return on BTC or ETH
- One risk disclosure: what the risks are, honestly
- A calendar invite for when the beta opens

**First session in the app:**

Screen 1 — Welcome: "Let's connect your crypto. Where do you hold it right now?" → Coinbase / Binance / Kraken / I have a wallet already

Screen 2 — Connect: One-tap connection to the selected exchange. No wallet addresses visible. "We're reading your BTC and ETH balance — we won't touch anything without your approval."

Screen 3 — Your portfolio: "You have 0.04 BTC and 0.5 ETH. At current rates, you could be earning approximately X% per year. Want to get started?"

Screen 4 — First action: "Deposit your BTC to start earning. Here's what happens: [three steps, plain English]. Here's what you'll earn. Here's how to get it back." One big button: "Start earning."

Screen 5 — Confirmation: "Your BTC is now earning yield. You'll see your balance grow here. You can withdraw any time." Display a real-time earnings counter.

That's it. No swaps, no borrows, no bridges, no recipient selection. Jordan's first job is to see her money earning something. Everything else comes later.

## Patterns to extract (for framework feedback)

- The "broken current experience" walkthrough (showing the multi-step painful path before showing the solution) is highly effective for DeFi personas. Consider this as a standard discovery pattern for any product with complex existing workflows.
- The gap between marketing copy ("no wrapping") and product UI (WBTC labelling) is a class of inconsistency that erodes trust faster than it was built. A "promise/proof" audit step should be added to the framework — checking that every claim made in marketing is visible and consistent in the product.
- Persona-specific user type labelling on the website (Traders, Stakers, Degens) fails to include the primary addressable audience (passive holders). This suggests a framework check: "Does every target segment see themselves in the product's own language?"
- The yield/rate gap — failing to show numbers on a financial product — is a near-universal error in DeFi marketing. A framework heuristic: any product whose value proposition is financial returns must show a number, even approximate, before asking for sign-up.
