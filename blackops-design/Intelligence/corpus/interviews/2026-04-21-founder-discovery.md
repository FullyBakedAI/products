## Turn 1 — subject

> My name is Ant Ludlow. I am a design leader, primarily targeting financial services. I have been doing this for 25 years. I have worked at various different institutions, startups. I have experience across all aspects of design from brand, marketing, product, digital transformation, launch. I created two businesses, Fathom and Bullet. In Fathom I used to help establish tier 1 to tier 3 investment banks get off the ground and tech providers like Market Access, TradeWeb. So I've got a heritage of doing work in that field, but the main thing that we did at Fathom was we helped startups get off the ground — early stage fintechs that would come in to [us], they'd get their investment and then they would need to launch, and [we would] help every aspect of their companies. And what they usually didn't understand is that design is really important to that, so we would help them with brand, marketing, positioning, we create the products both design and build, and then we'd help launch them into the market and make them successful. I probably helped nine fintechs to get off the ground and out of those, I think four became unicorns — so Axis Fintech, Pyram, OpenGamma, Mosaic Smart Data, BlockX, Talos — and there was a few others. Capitec. I can't remember any others offhand but that gives you a bit of an example.
>
> So what do I want to achieve here and what matters to me right now — I am launching a new business called BlackOps. The reason it's called BlackOps is because we're going to put together a very select elite group of specialists to help businesses, to help them drive the change that they didn't necessarily know that they needed, to help bake in brand and amazing design and AI-first tech across their businesses. And I want to do this on behalf of VCs. So VCs at the moment, a lot of their portfolio dies on the vine. And I already have a track record of creating opportunity for these early stage businesses and launching them in ways that they couldn't have done before making sure that they succeed. My idea is to create this on behalf of a VC, so it essentially becomes a venture studio that they own and that we would take an equity cut in the businesses that we helped to launch.
>
> A company comes to them and asks them for $5 million and they often think that they want $5 million for marketing, for tech. We're going to show them that through an AI-first approach that we can actually create all the narrative around the business. We can create amazing stories. We can bring it to life in a way that will drive adoption. And then we're going to be creating this for an AI framework that underpins BlackOps, that we will go through each different stage and we will help them to do either deep research or design or build a product. And then we will actually give them these tools so they can use them themselves as a leave behind.

## Turn 3 — subject

> I've watched one recently where they thought that they knew everything. They had a good idea, but the reality is that they didn't know how to create that idea, how to build momentum and how to drive adoption. So they ended up scattergunning a thin slither across a business rather than really nailing something that's compelling.
>
> BlackOps is there to create differentiated experiences, to create compelling experiences, and that really is the difference between a business being successful or not. And that is why the companies that I helped launch — we differentiated them in the market. They weren't just addressing the problem, they were addressing a problem in a compelling way.
>
> I think when you don't do that it's very easy to just let things drift and end up with kind of tech debt and slop.

## Turn 4 — interviewer playback + probe on "compelling"

Failure shape captured:
- Founder thinks they already know what to do
- Decent idea, can't execute it into something that lands
- Spreads too thin — "scattergunning a slither"
- Drift sets in → tech debt → slop

Counter-move captured: **BlackOps forces compelling differentiation** — the thesis Ant credits Fathom's portfolio wins to.

**Probe on the load-bearing word:** *When you see a product or brand that's compelling vs. one that isn't — what's the actual tell? Not the textbook definition — the thing that, in the first ten seconds, makes you think "yes, this is it" or "no, this isn't." And is "compelling" one specific thing for you, or a bundle of things you've learned to recognize together over 25 years?*
