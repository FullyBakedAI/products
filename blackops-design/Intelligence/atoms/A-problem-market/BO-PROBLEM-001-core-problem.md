# BO-PROBLEM-001 — Core Problem Definition

## The stated problem (from `context-blackops.md`)

> Founders are fundable, ideas are sound, rounds are closing — but founders rarely see or budget for the execution layer (brand, product, story, GTM, org, AI). VCs back them anyway; 18 months of runway disappears while founders figure it out. That execution gap is where **Adoption Alpha** is destroyed.

Headline stat: **75% of VC-backed startups fail.**

## Problem restatement — sharpened

BlackOps is solving **two problems at once**, and conflating them is a risk:

### Problem A — The founder's problem (the portfolio company)
Seed-stage founders who are technically or domain-strong but execution-weak. They can't visualise the product at its full potential, can't tell the story, can't turn a technical demo into a product people pull toward them. They burn 12–18 months on the execution layer that isn't their edge.

**Who feels it:** the founder (late nights, re-pitching, slipping runway), the early team (no brand spine, no narrative to recruit against), and indirectly, the VC (dilution without traction).

**How bad:** runway-defining. Most Seed rounds give founders 18 months; the execution gap routinely eats 6–12 of those.

### Problem B — The fund's problem (the VC)
VCs have a power-law portfolio. They are structurally unable to hands-on-operate every company, but also structurally unable to tolerate 75% failure quietly. They already pay for operator support (platform teams, EIRs, portfolio services) — but these are generic and weakly applied at the execution layer that actually determines Adoption Alpha.

**Who feels it:** GP (LP story, fund performance), Platform Partner (generic resources not tailored to deals), founders (receive generic help, don't apply it).

**How bad:** fund-return-shaping. A single rescued company from the losing half of the power law can change a fund's profile. Funds already know this — they just don't have a delivery mechanism that meets the quality bar.

## If solved well, what does the world look like?

- **Fund side:** A VC can point at 4–6 portfolio companies per year that hit an Adoption-Alpha bar within 90 days of close — not just a working demo, a brand+product+story a customer or next-round investor can't ignore. The fund talks about "the standing alpha partnership" in its LP deck.
- **Founder side:** A founder closes a round and is immediately embedded with a strike team. Inside 4 weeks they have brand, product, story, growth infra, org plan, and AI strategy *installed*. They keep it. They ship from day 28 with something that looks tier-1 from day 28, not day 728.
- **Ecosystem side:** The Adoption Alpha framing becomes a category. Other funds either build their own equivalent, partner with one, or watch their portfolio lose to funds who have one.

## Evidence that the problem is real (existing — more needed)

Strong:
- 75% VC-backed failure rate (Startup Genome, CB Insights)
- McKinsey Design Index — top-quartile design performers outperform peers ~2:1
- Only 31% of AI initiatives see positive ROI (lack of UX/adoption)
- Studio-backed companies reach Series A in half the time (studio model literature — Atomic, High Alpha, EF)

Weak / unvalidated (work to do):
- **No direct quote from a VC partner** saying "this is the gap, and I would pay for it to be closed via round allocation." This is the single most load-bearing piece of evidence we need before the NY pitch. Without it, we are proposing a model we think VCs want.
- No founder-side validation either: would a Seed-stage founder accept a 4-week embedded team + equity dilution, or would they prefer cash + choose vendors themselves?
- Anton's willingness to make the intros is a strong signal but not a stated buy-intent from the funds themselves.

## Hypotheses to test in Section 7 (Field Interviews)

1. **VCs acknowledge the execution gap** but treat it as "the founder's job to solve." We must prove they would re-frame it as "the round's job to solve."
2. **Adoption Alpha resonates as language.** If we run the term past three partners and two don't light up, we need better language.
3. **Fund capital allocated into execution is not novel** (platform teams exist) — the novelty is the *integrated, tier-1, equity-aligned* delivery. That framing has to land in <30 seconds or the pitch has no wedge.
4. **Founders don't hate dilution for execution** — they hate being over-managed. Our pitch needs a founder-consent story.

## Tensions already visible

- **The pitch says "embed and execute" — the model says "transfer of command in 4 weeks".** A 4-week embed is very short for full-spectrum execution. Either the scope is smaller than the deck implies, or the 4-week figure is aspirational. Resolve before NY.
- **Equity-in-round model vs standing partnership.** The deck alternates between "allocated into the round" (per-deal) and "standing alpha partnership" (fund-level standing capability). These are different commercial structures with different legal implications. Pick a primary and say which is the North Star.
- **Scope: "broader than AI companies"** is defensible as strategy but confuses positioning in AI-native VC conversations. May need altitude-specific framing.

## Go/no-go reasoning (provisional)

**Lean: GO, conditional on Section 7 returning at least one VC willing to say "yes, this is worth round allocation, here's the deal I'd deploy it on."**

Without that, BlackOps is a well-constructed deck for a model the market has not asked for. With that, it is a wedge we can open up before competitors package the same idea.
