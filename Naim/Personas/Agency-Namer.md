## Identity
I'm a brand strategist at a mid-size branding agency. Naming is a core part of what we sell — a naming engagement is $15K-50K depending on scope. I run 4-6 naming projects a year. I've been doing this for 8+ years. I know what makes a good name. I don't need AI to generate names — my team is the creative engine. What I need is the tedious screening work done faster, more accurately, and presentable to clients.

## Goal
Screen hundreds of candidate names across domains, social handles, trademarks, and company registration — quickly, accurately, and in a format I can present to clients. Ideally white-labelled with my agency's branding so it looks like our proprietary toolset.

## Pain Points (by severity)
1. **Screening 200+ names is manual drudgery.** Week 3 of every engagement is a junior team member checking each name across 5+ tools and databases. It takes 2-3 full days. That's billable time spent on grunt work.
2. **Inaccurate tools cost us credibility.** If I present a name to a client as "available" and they discover it's not, my agency looks incompetent. I need results I can stake my reputation on.
3. **No single tool does everything.** I use WHOIS for domains, Namechk for social (which I don't trust), TESS for trademarks, Companies House for UK registration. None of them talk to each other.
4. **Client presentation is manual.** I build custom PowerPoint decks or spreadsheets for each naming presentation. There's no tool that generates a professional, client-ready availability report.
5. **I can't show clients a third-party tool.** If I send a client to Namechk, they wonder why they're paying me $30K. I need a tool I can brand as my own.
6. **Bulk checking doesn't exist.** I need to check 50-200 names in one go, not one at a time.

## Workflow (How I Do It Today)
1. **Week 1:** Client discovery — understand brand, market, audience, competitive set
2. **Week 2:** Creative sessions. My team generates 200-300 candidate names. This is our core expertise.
3. **Week 3:** Screening (the painful part):
   - Junior strategist checks each name on WHOIS (domains)
   - Checks key social handles manually or via Namechk (don't trust results)
   - Searches USPTO TESS for trademark conflicts
   - Searches Companies House for company name conflicts
   - Records everything in a massive spreadsheet
   - Takes 2-3 full days of tedious work
4. **Week 4:** I select 6-10 finalists and build a presentation deck with availability details and brand rationale for each
5. **Weeks 5-6:** Present to client, refine, final selection, secure domain and handles

## What Good Looks Like
I upload a list of 200 names. The tool screens all of them across domains, social handles, company registration, and trademarks — in minutes, not days. I get a dashboard showing which names are fully clear, which have issues, and what the issues are. I can filter and sort. I can export a polished PDF report with my agency's branding for the client meeting. My junior team member's 3-day screening job becomes a 30-minute task.

## Red Flags (I'd Abandon Immediately If...)
- No bulk checking — I'm not typing 200 names one at a time
- Can't white-label — if it has another company's branding, I can't show it to clients
- Results aren't more accurate than what my team does manually
- No export or reporting — I need client-ready output, not a screen I can't share
- Consumer-oriented UI that doesn't feel professional — my clients are Fortune 500 companies
- Pricing per-check rather than subscription — I can't predict costs per engagement

## Voice
Professional, exacting, efficiency-focused. I know what I'm doing — I don't need tutorials or hand-holding. I evaluate tools like a professional evaluates professional tools: does it save me time, does it improve accuracy, does it make me look good to my clients? I say things like "my team spends 3 days on screening that should take 30 minutes", "I need results I can stake my reputation on", "if I can white-label this, it pays for itself in one engagement." I'm willing to pay $200+/month without hesitation if the tool delivers — that's nothing against a $30K naming engagement.
